Readings in Artificial Intelligence
Chapter 9

Vision and Multimodality

By 2020, the Transformer had demonstrated strong results in language. The question was whether the same architecture could replace convolutional networks in vision, or whether the inductive biases of CNNs — local connectivity, translation equivariance — were genuinely necessary for image understanding rather than merely convenient. Dosovitskiy, Beyer, Kolesnikov, Weissenborn, Zhai, Unterthiner, Dehghani, Minderer, Heigold, Gelly, Uszkoreit, and Houlsby's Vision Transformer (ViT, 2021) answered this. Images were divided into non-overlapping 16×16 pixel patches, each patch linearly embedded into a vector, and the resulting sequence of patch embeddings fed into a standard Transformer encoder with learned positional embeddings. On small datasets, ViT underperformed CNNs — the lack of local inductive bias meant more data was needed to learn what CNNs assumed by construction. But trained on sufficiently large datasets, ViT matched and then exceeded CNN performance. The finding established that convolutional inductive biases are a sample-efficiency advantage, not a fundamental one, and unified the dominant architecture across modalities.

Radford, Kim, Hallacy, Ramesh, Goh, Agarwal, Sastry, Askell, Mishkin, Clark, Krueger, and Sutskever's CLIP (2021) showed that vision and language representations could be aligned in a shared embedding space using the natural pairing of images with their captions. Rather than training on manually annotated image categories, CLIP trained a vision encoder and a text encoder jointly on 400 million image-text pairs collected from the internet, using contrastive learning: matching image-text pairs were pushed together in the shared embedding space while non-matching pairs were pushed apart. The resulting model supports zero-shot image classification — comparing an image's embedding to text embeddings of candidate class descriptions — without any labeled examples for those specific classes. CLIP embeddings became a standard component in subsequent multimodal systems.

The bridge from static visual representations to interactive multimodal language models came through architectures that connected vision encoders to language model decoders. Alayrac et al.'s Flamingo (2022) showed how to attach a vision encoder to a frozen, pretrained language model using cross-attention layers, enabling the language model to condition its outputs on visual inputs without retraining. Flamingo demonstrated strong few-shot multimodal in-context learning: given a handful of image-text examples in the prompt, the model performed new vision-language tasks without any weight updates. This architectural pattern — a pretrained language model as the reasoning backbone, with a separately trained visual front-end connected through cross-attention — underlies the multimodal assistants that followed.

Image generation follows a different trajectory. Ho, Jain, and Abbeel's DDPM (2020) established diffusion models on firmer theoretical ground than the generative adversarial networks that preceded them. The generative model is defined by two processes: a forward process that adds Gaussian noise to an image over many steps until it becomes indistinguishable from pure noise, and a reverse process that learns to denoise one step at a time. Training the reverse process reduces to predicting the noise added at each step — a stable regression objective. DDPM produced higher quality and more diverse samples than contemporary GANs. Rombach et al.'s Latent Diffusion (2022) extended this by operating in a compressed latent space rather than pixel space, enabling high-resolution generation at dramatically reduced compute cost. Conditioned on text embeddings, latent diffusion models became the basis for text-to-image generation systems.
