Readings in Artificial Intelligence
Chapter 11

Harms, Bias & Responsibility

Technical capability and responsible deployment are not the same thing. As AI systems moved from research settings into products and infrastructure, a body of empirical and analytical work made visible a set of harms that the rapid pace of development had tended to obscure. Two kinds of evidence accumulated in parallel: empirical audits demonstrating that deployed systems produce systematically disparate outcomes across demographic groups, and analytical arguments that the structural conditions of large-scale AI development make such outcomes likely to recur and difficult to remedy.

Joy Buolamwini and Timnit Gebru's "Gender Shades" (2018) audited commercial face analysis systems from Microsoft, IBM, and Face++ for accuracy across demographic subgroups defined by gender and skin tone. Error rates on darker-skinned women were up to 34.7 percentage points higher than on lighter-skinned men. The disparity arose from training datasets skewed toward lighter-skinned subjects, and it was present in systems actively sold and deployed in commercial and government contexts. The paper's methodological contribution was significant: it operationalized intersectional demographic subgroups rather than relying on aggregate accuracy, exposing failures that aggregate benchmarks would have obscured. Emma Strubell, Ananya Ganesh, and Andrew McCallum's "Energy and Policy Considerations" (2019) made a different cost legible: training a single large NLP model with neural architecture search produced estimated CO₂ emissions comparable to the lifetime emissions of five average American cars — an environmental externality that had been entirely absent from research reporting.

Emily Bender, Timnit Gebru, Angelina McMillan-Major, and Shmargaret Shmitchell's "On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots" (2021) synthesized these and related concerns into a structural argument against uncritical scaling. Three risks were identified. First, environmental cost scales with model size, and the incentive structure of the field rewards capability gains without penalizing compute expenditure; the institutions with the most resources to train large models are not those who bear the environmental consequences most directly. Second, models trained on internet text encode the biases, stereotypes, and exclusions present in that text — and larger training sets may simply mean more of the same distributions at greater scale, rather than broader or more representative coverage. Third, the fluency of language model outputs creates an illusion of meaning: outputs are mistaken for understanding, making systems easier to anthropomorphize, harder to scrutinize critically, and more likely to be deployed in high-stakes settings without adequate evaluation.

The paper was accepted to FAccT 2021. Timnit Gebru was dismissed from Google before it was published, in circumstances that became part of the field's history as much as the paper itself. The episode surfaced structural tensions between commercial incentives and critical research that remain present in the field.
