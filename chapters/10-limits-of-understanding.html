Readings in Artificial Intelligence
Chapter 10

Evaluation and Limits

As language model capabilities expanded, so did the difficulty of measuring them. Benchmarks developed to track progress tended to saturate quickly — models achieved near-human performance on tasks that had been considered representative of intelligence — without resolving whether they understood anything or had learned to exploit statistical regularities in test distributions. The measurement problem is not incidental: what the field believes about model capabilities depends entirely on how it chooses to evaluate them.

Emily Bender and Alexander Koller's "Climbing Towards NLU" (2020) drew a precise distinction between form — the observable linguistic signal, the sequence of tokens a model processes — and meaning — the relation between a linguistic expression and something outside language: a communicative intent, a state of the world. A model trained on text learns distributional regularities over form. It cannot, by this argument, learn meaning, because meaning requires grounding in the world that text alone does not provide. The paper's thought experiment is widely cited: imagine an entity that intercepts transmissions between two people communicating via radio, learns to participate fluently in the exchange, but has no access to the referents that give the transmitted forms meaning. Such an entity could achieve near-perfect linguistic form without any access to meaning. This criterion — not fluency but grounding — provides a precise standard against which to evaluate progress claims, and it has not been resolved by subsequent scaling.

Gary Marcus's "Deep Learning: A Critical Appraisal" (2018) approached evaluation from a different angle. Writing at the height of enthusiasm following AlphaGo and the first wave of large-scale NLP models, Marcus catalogued specific empirical limitations: fragility under distribution shift, poor sample efficiency, weak compositional generalization, the absence of causal reasoning, and difficulty integrating prior knowledge. The paper did not argue that deep learning had failed — it argued that the field had overclaimed, and that remaining gaps were unlikely to close through scale and data alone. As a forward-looking evaluation of the paradigm's limits, it established a checklist that subsequent research could be measured against.

The difficulty of evaluation at scale is documented in the empirical literature. Liang et al.'s HELM (2022) assembled a unified evaluation across many models and scenarios, providing a structured framework for comparative assessment across dimensions including accuracy, calibration, robustness, fairness, and efficiency. Srivastava et al.'s BIG-bench (2022) collected hundreds of tasks designed to probe specific capabilities and limitations — particularly those not represented in existing benchmarks. Together, these works reflect a sustained effort to move evaluation beyond single-task accuracy toward structured assessment of the diverse capabilities and failure modes of large language models — and a recognition that the choice of what to measure shapes the conclusions drawn.
