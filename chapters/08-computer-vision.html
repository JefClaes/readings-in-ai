Readings in Artificial Intelligence
Chapter 8

Computer Vision at Scale

By 2020, the Transformer had demonstrated strong results in language. The question was whether the same architecture could replace convolutional networks in vision, or whether the inductive biases of CNNs — local connectivity, translation equivariance — were genuinely necessary for image understanding rather than merely convenient. Dosovitskiy et al.'s Vision Transformer (ViT, 2020) answered this. Images were split into non-overlapping 16×16 pixel patches, each patch linearly embedded into a vector, and the resulting sequence of patch embeddings fed into a standard Transformer encoder with learned positional embeddings. On small datasets, ViT underperformed CNNs — the lack of local inductive bias meant more data was needed to learn what CNNs assumed by construction. But trained on large enough datasets, ViT matched and then exceeded CNN performance. The finding established that convolutional inductive biases are a sample-efficiency advantage, not a fundamental one, and unified the dominant architecture across vision and language.

Radford et al.'s CLIP (2021) addressed a different question: could image and text representations be learned jointly, using the natural pairing of images and their captions as supervision? Rather than training on manually annotated image categories, CLIP trained a vision encoder and a text encoder jointly on 400 million image-text pairs collected from the internet, using contrastive learning: matching image-text pairs were pushed close together in a shared embedding space while non-matching pairs were pushed apart. The resulting model supports zero-shot image classification — comparing an image's embedding to text embeddings of candidate class descriptions — without any labeled examples for those classes. CLIP embeddings also generalize broadly to downstream vision tasks, and the vision encoder has become a standard component in multimodal systems, including image generation models that use text prompts to guide generation.

Generating images rather than understanding them poses a different challenge. Prior to 2020, generative adversarial networks (GANs) were the dominant approach: a generator network produces images while a discriminator network attempts to distinguish generated from real images, and the two are trained adversarially. GANs could produce high-quality images but were difficult to train stably and prone to mode collapse — producing a limited variety of outputs regardless of input. Ho et al.'s DDPM (2020) established an alternative on firmer theoretical ground. The generative model is defined by two processes: a forward process that adds small amounts of Gaussian noise to an image over T steps until it becomes approximately pure noise, and a reverse process that learns to denoise one step at a time, starting from pure noise and recovering a plausible image. Training the reverse process reduces to predicting the noise added at each step, which is a stable regression objective. DDPM produced higher-quality and more diverse samples than contemporary GANs. The architecture became the basis for subsequent image generation systems, which extended it with conditioning on text embeddings (enabling text-to-image generation) and latent diffusion (operating in a compressed latent space for efficiency).
