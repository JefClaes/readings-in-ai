Chapter 3

Language Before Transformers

Unlike images — which have a natural dense representation as pixel values — words are discrete symbols with no inherent geometric structure. A one-hot encoding places every word at equal distance from every other, discarding all semantic information. For a neural network to reason about language, it needs a representation in which related words are close, and the model can generalize from words it has seen to words it has not seen in a given context.

Bengio, Ducharme, Vincent, and Jauvin's "A Neural Probabilistic Language Model" (2003) provided the first neural solution to this problem at scale. The model learns a dense vector — an embedding — for each word in the vocabulary, jointly with the parameters of a neural network that uses these embeddings to predict the probability of each word given its preceding context. The core innovation is not the language model itself — n-gram statistical models preceded it — but the learned representations. Because the model is trained to predict word contexts, words that appear in similar contexts receive similar embeddings. Grammatical relationships and some semantic ones become geometric: the model generalizes to unseen word sequences by composing the embeddings of known words rather than requiring each specific sequence to have appeared during training. This generalization capacity is what distinguishes neural language models from their statistical predecessors.

Mikolov et al.'s Word2Vec (2013) showed that the distributional idea could be pushed further with a dedicated shallow network and massive scale. Embeddings trained to predict context words — or to predict the center word from context — acquired compositional structure: "king" minus "man" plus "woman" yielded a vector near "queen." This arithmetic of meaning emerged from no supervision beyond raw text. Static word embeddings became a standard preprocessing step across NLP for several years. Their limitation was that word meaning is static: "bank" receives the same embedding whether it appears next to "river" or "funds." Peters et al.'s ELMo (2018) introduced contextual embeddings — representations produced by a bidirectional LSTM that vary with the full sentence, capturing polysemy that fixed vectors cannot. ELMo was a direct precursor to BERT, which replaced the LSTM with a Transformer and trained contextual representations with a masked prediction objective.

The sequence modeling problem — how to process inputs of varying length while capturing long-range dependencies — was addressed by recurrent networks. Hochreiter and Schmidhuber's LSTM (1997) introduced gating mechanisms that allowed hidden states to persist or reset selectively, enabling networks to learn which information to carry forward across many steps. Sutskever, Vinyals, and Le's Seq2Seq (2014) applied recurrent networks to tasks that map one sequence to another — translation, summarization — by encoding an input into a fixed-length hidden state and decoding it into an output. The architecture worked, but the compression bottleneck it required — representing an entire input in a single fixed-size vector before decoding began — caused quality to degrade on long inputs. This bottleneck directly motivated the attention mechanism described in the next chapter.
