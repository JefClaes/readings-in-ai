Chapter 5

Pre-training & Fine-tuning

The Transformer provided an architecture that scaled. The next question was how to train it. Prior to 2018, building a model for a specific NLP task meant training largely from scratch on labeled data for that task — sentiment labels, question-answer pairs, entailment annotations. Labeled data was expensive to collect, so models were small and narrow. Word embeddings might be pre-trained on unlabeled text, but the rest of the model was task-specific. Generalizing across tasks required training separate models.

Devlin et al.'s BERT (2018) demonstrated a different approach: pre-train a large Transformer on a self-supervised objective over massive unlabeled text, then fine-tune the entire model on a downstream task. The pre-training objective was masked language modeling (MLM): randomly mask 15% of input tokens and train the model to predict them from context. Because the model must use context from both directions to predict a masked token, it learns deeply bidirectional representations — an understanding of how each word relates to everything before and after it. A second objective, next sentence prediction, encouraged learning inter-sentence relationships. Fine-tuned on 11 NLP benchmarks — question answering, entailment, named entity recognition, and others — BERT set state-of-the-art on all of them simultaneously, often by large margins. The key insight was that most of the relevant knowledge about language could be obtained from unlabeled text alone.

OpenAI's GPT-2 (2019) explored a different architecture and objective. Where BERT is an encoder trained with a masked objective, GPT-2 is a decoder trained autoregressively: predict the next token from all preceding tokens. This produces a generative model rather than a representation model. The paper's central observation was about what emerged from scale and data diversity: a GPT-2 model trained on 40 gigabytes of web text demonstrated surprising capability at tasks it was never explicitly trained for — summarization, translation, question answering — when those tasks were framed as text completion. The model was not fine-tuned on these tasks at all. Capability was latent in the pre-trained weights and accessible through prompting.

BERT established the pre-training paradigm that defines modern language models and raised a question that the next chapter addresses directly: what happens as models and datasets get larger? OpenAI's GPT-2 (2019) explored the autoregressive alternative — a decoder trained to predict the next token rather than a masked one — and found that scale and data diversity alone produced surprising zero-shot capability on tasks the model was never trained for. BERT-style encoders and GPT-style decoders remain the two dominant pretraining architectures: the former for classification and retrieval tasks that require understanding a complete input; the latter for generation.
