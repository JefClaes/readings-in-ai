Chapter 7

Alignment

A language model trained to predict the next token is not trained to be a useful assistant. It is trained to be a good predictor of human-generated text — which includes text that is harmful, contradictory, and unhelpful. When prompted to answer a question, a well-trained language model may produce a plausible-sounding continuation of a question-answering format rather than a correct answer. When asked to explain something, it may produce fluent but confidently wrong text. The model is optimizing for distributional fidelity to its training corpus, not for helpfulness to a user.

The gap between predicting text and following instructions became apparent as GPT-3-class models were deployed. Models would ignore instructions, generate harmful content when prompted in certain ways, and confidently assert falsehoods. Scaling made the models more capable but did not automatically make them more aligned with what users wanted. A different kind of training was needed.

Ouyang et al.'s InstructGPT (2022) introduced a three-stage approach now known as RLHF — reinforcement learning from human feedback. First, human contractors wrote demonstrations of desired behavior for a range of prompts; the pre-trained model was fine-tuned on these via supervised learning. Second, pairs of model outputs for the same prompt were collected and human raters ranked them by quality; these preferences were used to train a reward model that predicted which outputs humans would prefer. Third, the supervised fine-tuned model was further trained using PPO — a reinforcement learning algorithm — with the reward model as the objective, subject to a KL-divergence penalty that prevented the policy from drifting too far from the supervised fine-tuned baseline.

The results were striking in two ways. Human raters substantially preferred InstructGPT outputs over those of the base GPT-3 model — across helpfulness, truthfulness, and harmlessness. More surprisingly, a 1.3 billion parameter InstructGPT model was preferred over the 175 billion parameter base GPT-3, despite being over 100 times smaller. Alignment training proved more efficient than raw scale for producing outputs that humans found useful. The paper also found that InstructGPT was less likely to generate harmful content and more likely to acknowledge uncertainty, though it remained imperfect on both counts.

RLHF has since become a standard component of large language model training. Every widely deployed assistant model uses some variant of this pipeline. The alignment problem it addresses — the gap between what a model is trained to do and what its developers and users want it to do — remains an active area of research, with ongoing work on reward model reliability, specification of preferences, and scalable oversight.
