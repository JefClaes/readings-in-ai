Chapter 7

Alignment

A language model trained to predict the next token is not trained to be helpful. It is trained to model the distribution of human-generated text — which includes harmful content, confident errors, and outputs that fulfill the letter of a prompt while ignoring its intent. The gap between what a model is trained to do and what its developers and users want it to do is the alignment problem at the scale of language models.

Christiano, Leike, Brown, Martic, Legg, and Amodei's "Deep Reinforcement Learning from Human Preferences" (2017) established the conceptual foundation for what is now called reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF). The paper addressed a general problem: how do you train a reinforcement learning agent to maximize a goal that humans can recognize but cannot easily specify as a reward function? Their solution was to infer the reward signal from human preferences expressed as pairwise comparisons. Given two candidate behaviors, a human rater selects the one they prefer; a reward model is trained to predict these preferences; and the agent is trained with RL to maximize the learned reward. This separates the problem of specifying what is wanted from the problem of optimizing for it — a separation that proves essential when the target behavior is complex and contextual.

Applied to language models at production scale, Ouyang et al.'s InstructGPT (2022) demonstrated that this framework makes models substantially more useful. Human contractors ranked pairs of model outputs by quality across a broad prompt distribution; the reward model was trained on these rankings; and a GPT-3-scale model was fine-tuned using proximal policy optimization with the learned reward. Human raters substantially preferred InstructGPT outputs to those of the base GPT-3 model — including a 1.3 billion parameter InstructGPT over the 175 billion parameter base — establishing that alignment training is a more efficient path to useful outputs than raw scale. The result also found that aligned models were less likely to generate harmful content and more likely to acknowledge uncertainty.

Two subsequent approaches challenged the dependence on human raters and reinforcement learning as the mechanism. Bai et al.'s Constitutional AI (2022) proposed training models to critique and revise their own outputs according to a set of stated principles, using another language model as the feedback provider rather than humans — substituting AI feedback for human feedback and enabling scaling of the oversight process. Rafailov et al.'s Direct Preference Optimization (2023) reframed the RLHF objective entirely: by deriving a closed-form relationship between the optimal policy and the reward function, DPO eliminates the need to train an explicit reward model and run RL at all, instead fine-tuning directly on preference data with a supervised objective. The progression from Christiano through InstructGPT, Constitutional AI, and DPO represents a field iterating on both the mechanism of alignment and the source of feedback — from human pairwise comparisons, to AI-generated critiques, to direct optimization against expressed preferences.
